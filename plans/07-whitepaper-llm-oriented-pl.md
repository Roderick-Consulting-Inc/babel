> *White paper, third installment of the Babel / Inflexión series, companion to* `04-whitepaper-babel.md` *(methodology) and* `05-whitepaper-inflexion.md` *(Spanish-grammar instance). Style: Chicago author-date via pandoc + BibTeX. Voice: essayistic for curious minds. Honest preparation by default — hedged novelty claims, lineage credited, counter-evidence cited.*

## 1. Two questions

The first installment of this series [@rodriguez_babel_2026] argued that the esoteric-programming-language field had produced a vast corpus and a vague methodology: roughly 1,500 catalogued languages and ~800 Brainfuck derivatives, with each author rebuilding the same scaffolding because no shared parameter schema existed. Babel proposed that schema. The second installment, *Inflexión* [@rodriguez_inflexion_2026], developed one specific instantiation — a hand-built esoteric programming language whose semantics flow from Rioplatense Argentine Spanish grammar — and joined a small lineage of inflection-driven non-English natural-language esolangs (Perligata, Espro, Tampio, Wenyan, Inflexión). Both papers are written *for humans*. The languages they discuss are evaluated by what they do for human readers, writers, and the small community of practitioners who care about esoteric programming languages as a research and cultural genre.

This third installment is about a parallel field that has emerged on a different schedule and for a different audience. Between Sun, Du, Yang, Li, and Lo's ISSTA 2024 paper *AI Coders Are Among Us* [@sun_simpy_2024] and the present moment, at least 53 distinct artifacts have been built whose target audience is *large language models*. SimPy [@sun_simpy_2024] strips Python's formatting tokens to cut LLM-input cost by 13.5% on CodeLlama and 10.4% on GPT-4. Pel [@mohammadi_pel_2025] designs a new programming language from scratch with a uniform grammar 'to facilitate easier learning (in-context) and reliable generation by LLMs.' Quasar [@mcaleese_quasar_2025] reports 42% time and 52% security improvements on ViperGPT with a lambda-calculus-cored language optimised for LLM-agent code actions. LLMON [@hind_llmon_2026] proposes an LLM-native markup at the structured-data interface. The LMPL workshop at SPLASH — *Language Models and Programming Languages* — held its first edition in Singapore in 2025 and is accepting submissions for its second edition in Oakland for October 2026 [@lmpl_2026_cfp]. The field has a name and a venue.

The pace and the scale together raise two questions, both of which this paper takes seriously.

**The first question is the design question:**

> *What does the most efficient programming language look like, considering it needs to be understood, written, vetted, or at least auditable by humans; easy to understand and implement by machines; and easy for LLMs to read and generate?*

The question's structure matters. "Most efficient" is not a single-axis optimisation. SimPy is most efficient on *LLM-token-economy-of-Python-code*. Inflexión is most efficient on *morphological-density-of-grammar-derived-semantics*. APL [@iverson_notation_1980] is most efficient on *expression-density-per-glyph*. Python is most efficient on *human-author-velocity-with-tooling-ecosystem*. None dominates the others on all axes. The design question reduces, honestly stated, to: *which corner of a multi-stakeholder Pareto frontier is worth occupying, and how would one know?*

**The second question, equally load-bearing, is the gating question:**

> *Does building a new programming language actually add value over the existing positions on the frontier — Python plus SimPy, Pel, Quasar, LLMON, and the rest — or would the exercise reduce to pretending to be cool with accepted techs?*

This is the honest-preparation discipline applied to the project itself. Installment 05's lineage correction [@rodriguez_inflexion_2026 §1] reframed Inflexión from *first ever* to *fifth in a small lineage* because a pre-publication literature check surfaced four prior authors. The same discipline applies here, one level up: the decision to *exist as a new language* is something the empirical work must justify, not something that exists by default. The null result is real and acceptable. If the empirical comparison shows existing artifacts already cover the Pareto frontier on the chosen-stakeholder profile, the right outcome is to ship this paper as a methodology contribution and stop. No new language. The temptation to add another entry to a 53-artifact field because the surrounding research is interesting is exactly the trap to avoid.

The methodology proposed here is the framework that makes the design choices explicit and the gating decision empirically grounded. Babel's parameter schema, applied to the LLM-oriented design space, gives the field a shared vocabulary for what it has been varying implicitly. The empirical cascade laid out in §7 lets a researcher answer the gating question in cost order — cheapest measurements first, expensive measurements only on candidates that survive — and report the result honestly, with explicit gaps where measurements were skipped.

## 2. The field as it stands, 2024–2026

A pre-publication literature pass surfaced 53 artifacts in the LLM-oriented programming-language design space, with broad coverage across arXiv, ACL Anthology, ACM venues, the LMPL workshop programmes, the IBM and Stanford open-source repositories, and the practitioner ecosystem. The full bibliography is in the references; the structural observation worth making here is that **two distinct families have been converging under the same banner**.

The first family is *language LLMs write* — programming languages whose grammar has been redesigned so the language model itself generates code more cheaply, more reliably, or both. SimPy [@sun_simpy_2024] is the foundational example. Token Sugar [@sun_token_sugar_2025] generalises the move to other languages. The Hidden Cost of Readability [@pan_hiddencost_2025] quantifies the formatting tax across multiple LLMs. ShortCoder [@liu_shortcoder_2026] does syntax-level optimisation with rule-based rewriting plus LLM-guided refinement. NanoLang, B-IR, and ALaS are practitioner artifacts in the same direction: small languages whose author intent is *what an LLM will emit, not what a human will type*. Pel [@mohammadi_pel_2025] takes the further step of designing a new programming language from scratch under this constraint. Quasar [@mcaleese_quasar_2025] adds a lambda-calculus core and conformal-prediction reliability bounds.

The second family is *language that runs LLMs* — programming languages whose programs *call* language models as part of their semantics. LMQL [@lmql_2024] is the grandparent. DSPy [@dspy_2025] adds typed signatures and a compiler-style optimiser. SGLang [@sglang_2025] is co-designed PL plus runtime. APPL [@dong_appl_2025] embeds prompts in Python with an async runtime. PDL [@vaziri_pdl_2025] is IBM's YAML-based declarative prompt language. Plang [@hu_plang_2025] blends natural-language prompt with control-flow markers. Pangolin [@tan_pangolin_2025] makes best-of-N and retries first-class via the selection monad. Effect-handler-based prompt programming [@wang_lmpl_2025] separates LLM-script logic from effects. These languages do not strip or redesign anything for the *target* LLM; they design infrastructure for *invoking* LLMs.

Adjacent to both families, but conceptually distinct, is the markup-and-data-format axis. LLMON [@hind_llmon_2026], TOON [@toon_spec_2025], and PDL [@vaziri_pdl_2025] target the structured-data boundary rather than the executable-code boundary. Tokenese [@youvan_tokenese_2025] takes the move all the way out of programming and proposes a *natural language* designed for AI tokenization. JSON-vs-YAML-vs-Markdown-vs-TOON practitioner benchmarks [@hannecke_format_2025] measure the *output*-side formatting tax that complements SimPy's *input*-side observation.

Grammar-constrained and type-constrained decoding is the silent PL-design lever of 2025. XGrammar [@dong_xgrammar_2024], Type-Constrained Code Generation [@mundler_type_constrained_2025] at PLDI 2025, Grammar-Aligned Decoding [@park_grammar_aligned_2024], and the rest of this cluster do not call themselves "PL design," but a context-free grammar or type system attached to the decoder *is* a programming-language contract negotiated with the model. The field would be incomplete without these.

Finally, the position-paper wave: Du-Wang-Wang [@du_wang_wang_2025], CNL-P [@xing_cnlp_2025], MLDS [@mlds_2025], the *NL-aligned theorem prover* call by Cao, Xie, and Yan [@cao_nl_aligned_2025], the Kore *machine-native language* essay [@kore_machine_native_2025], and several Medium-published practitioner essays are all arguing variants of the same thesis in independent vocabularies: *programming-language design should be reshaped around LLM cognition rather than human cognition*. The thesis converges across disciplines without yet having a shared ontology.

Counter-evidence runs alongside the convergence. *Let Me Speak Freely* [@tam_let_me_speak_2024] shows that strict structured-output formats can *degrade* LLM reasoning quality — the opposite of the SimPy-direction intuition. *Scaling Laws for Code* [@scaling_laws_code_2026] gives a predictability ordering across existing programming languages (roughly C# < Java ≈ Rust < Go < TypeScript < JavaScript < Python) and argues that language structure changes LLM scaling behaviour in measurable, predictable ways — suggesting that picking the *right existing language* might dominate inventing a new one. The *kirancodes* essay [@codes_mediocrity_2025] argues, from a critical position, that LLMs push programming-language design toward conservative, popular syntaxes rather than novel ones; the wider study of LLM programming-language preferences [@xu_llm_preferences_2025] supports the empirical half of that claim. The Bayazit-Li LMPL paper [@bayazit_proof_2025] examines a pre-existing programming language (a proof assistant) as an LLM target and informs the *PL-was-already-good-for-LLMs* axis the position-paper wave largely ignores.

The field, by 2026, contains at least 53 distinct artifacts across these axes, with no consensus framework, no shared evaluation methodology, and active counter-evidence that the field's central direction may be wrong. **That is the situation a methodology paper has to make sense of.**

## 3. The Youvan / Inflexión tension, resolved empirically

The simplest version of the field's central claim — *natural-language morphology is inefficient for AI tokenization, so AI-oriented languages should be more regular and more compact than natural-language-derived ones* — is what Youvan defends explicitly in *Tokenese* [@youvan_tokenese_2025] and what the SimPy direction assumes implicitly. The simplest counter — *natural-language morphology can pack semantic distinctions tighter than English-keyworded syntax, so engaging it is worth the tokenization cost* — is what Inflexión [@rodriguez_inflexion_2026 §3.1] embodies in the *ser* / *estar* mapping. *Ser* and *estar* together carry the immutable-versus-mutable binding distinction in a single morpheme that Python expresses (where it expresses it at all) through a five-token `Final[int] = 5` annotation, which is optional and not enforced at the binding site.

Both observations are correct on their narrow axes:

| | Natural-language morphology brings | |
|---|---|---|
| | **Wins** | **Losses** |
| For humans familiar with the language | Locally rich: one morpheme carries the whole distinction without a separate annotation | Irregularities, exceptions, dialect variance; learning curve for outsiders |
| For LLMs under byte-pair encoding | Frequent forms tokenize cheaply (one or two tokens); semantic density at the surface | Rare and inflected forms fragment into multi-token sequences [@morphbpe_2025]; BPE was trained on analytic-language frequency |

The net effect is empirical, not theoretical. Whether morphological density's local wins dominate its tokenizer-tail losses on a given workload depends on the workload, on the tokenizer's vocabulary, on the LLM's training-data exposure to the substrate language, and on which stakeholder's efficiency is being measured. The first installment of the empirical methodology proposed in §7 takes a small fixed program suite and tokenizes Python, SimPy-style Python, and Inflexión versions of each across six frontier tokenizers; preliminary results (averaged over five programs, six tokenizers) show Inflexión consuming **-28% tokens versus Python** with a per-program range of -61.8% to +8.9%. The result is suggestive, not conclusive — four of the five Inflexión samples use design-level syntax not yet implemented in the runtime, and the suite is too small to reject either direction's thesis. Conclusive results require the full empirical cascade [§7] run across a larger task suite with more LLM models. The point worth making in this section is structural: the Youvan position and the Inflexión position are not in conflict; they describe different points on the same two-dimensional trade-off. The field's open empirical question is which point wins on a given workload, not which position is right in the abstract.

The same point applies symmetrically to the wider field. SimPy's whitespace-stripping wins are measured on Python source; whether they transfer to a Rust or Haskell target is unstudied. Pel's uniform-grammar wins are measured on LLM-agent code-action workloads; whether they transfer to general programming is unstudied. Quasar's 42-percent-time / 52-percent-security wins are measured on ViperGPT and ScienceQA; whether they transfer to SWE-bench-style multi-step agent workloads is unstudied. Every existing artifact's central empirical claim is local to its measurement context, and the field has no shared cross-context benchmark. The methodology proposed in §6–§7 addresses exactly this gap.

## 4. Stakeholders, Tokens, Time

A programming language is consumed by many agents, each with its own notion of efficiency. The naïve framing — *one language is more efficient than another* — collapses if we do not specify *for whom*. This section unfolds the question along two complementary views: a structural inventory of nine stakeholders with their primary efficiency metrics, and an economic projection of those metrics onto two scarce resources.

The structural view is what design conversations need. A programming language is read and written and run by:

- **The human author**, whose efficiency is *time-to-express-an-idea* (keystrokes, cognitive load, distance from intention to working code). Secondary metrics: IDE-completion fit, muscle memory, refactor cost.
- **The human reviewer or auditor**, whose efficiency is *time-to-verify-correctness locally* — the ability to read a span of code and judge it correct without consulting global context. Secondary metrics: visual cues, semantic density per line, code-smell detectability.
- **The human maintainer**, whose efficiency is *time-to-debug-and-change* without breaking other things. Secondary metrics: error-message quality, stack-trace clarity, blast-radius of a change.
- **The compiler or interpreter**, whose efficiency is *parse complexity and AST-reasoning cost*. Secondary metrics: feasibility of static analysis, optimizability.
- **The static analyser, IDE, or language-server protocol**, whose efficiency is *inference feasibility* — can it infer types, resolve references, and offer correct completions without unbounded computation? Secondary metrics: incremental-update latency.
- **The LLM as consumer**, whose efficiency is *tokens per semantic unit and ambiguity load*. Secondary metrics: BPE-vocabulary alignment, in-context-learning ease.
- **The LLM as generator**, whose efficiency is *one-shot accuracy and correction cost*. Secondary metrics: syntax-error rate, grounding rate, retry budget.
- **Code-as-data systems** (indexers, embedding-based retrievers, automated code-modification tools), whose efficiency is *indexability and retrieval signal*. Secondary metrics: embedding-vector quality, AST-pattern matchability.
- **The security auditor** (human or automated), whose efficiency is *reasoning about effects and capability scope*. Secondary metrics: clarity of the sandbox model, locality of side-effect declaration.

Most existing programming languages optimise for one to three of these stakeholders and accept losses on the rest. Python optimises for human author plus human maintainer plus ecosystem; loses on LLM tokens and on glance-density. SimPy [@sun_simpy_2024] preserves Python's AST and the rest of Python's strengths while reclaiming the LLM-token loss; the cost is borne by the human reviewer, who now reads code without the formatting cues PEP 8 inserted. Pel [@mohammadi_pel_2025] optimises for LLM generator plus LLM consumer; gives up the human-ecosystem advantages a 30-year-old language accumulates. Inflexión optimises for morphological-density-as-research-artifact; gives up universal accessibility entirely, since the human-reviewer cost for a non-Spanish-speaker is unbounded. The first move of methodology is to *name the stakeholder one is optimising for*; opaque optimisation against an unnamed stakeholder is a recurring failure mode in the LLM-oriented PL literature.

The structural view's nine rows compress, for economic purposes, onto two scarce resources. **Tokens** are the LLM context-budget scarcity: input tokens plus output tokens, priced concretely per million tokens by every commercial LLM provider, counted trivially via the relevant tokenizer library. Every LLM stakeholder's efficiency reduces to Token consumption per task. **Time** is the universal scarcity for every non-LLM stakeholder: author-write-time, reviewer-read-time, auditor-verify-time, maintainer-debug-time, learner-onboard-time, machine-parse-time, machine-compile-time, machine-execute-time. Time is concretely measurable in seconds and pricable in dollars-per-hour at the relevant labour or compute rate. The trade-off between Tokens and Time is the central economic optimisation: a language with rich notation may save Tokens at the cost of human Time (learning curve, audit difficulty); a language with verbose human-friendly notation may save Time at the cost of Tokens (longer prompts, larger generation budgets). The Pareto frontier between Tokens and Time is what existing artifacts implicitly occupy, and what new artifacts have to find unoccupied space on.

A third axis sits beside Tokens and Time but is conceptually different: **Quality**. Correctness, security, robustness, and predictability are not resources to minimise. They are *constraints* the optimisation respects. The economic question is therefore: *minimise joint Tokens-plus-Time cost subject to Quality at or above a chosen threshold*. A language that wins on Tokens by stripping type information may lose Quality dramatically; the trade-off is real and has to be priced. *Let Me Speak Freely* [@tam_let_me_speak_2024] is the empirical reminder that the Quality constraint is binding: aggressive format restrictions can degrade LLM reasoning quality below the threshold worth accepting.

Each stakeholder dimension also has a *measurement difficulty*, and the difficulty distribution is highly skewed. Token counts are trivial — every tokenizer ships with a one-line API. Compile times are easy — instrument the toolchain. LLM-generator accuracy is medium — pass@1 on a controlled benchmark takes a few hundred API calls per language-by-model cell. Human-reviewer-time is hard — controlled comprehension-task user studies with timing. Human-maintainer-time is very hard — longitudinal real-codebase studies, or proxy via bug-fix-time on a held-out suite. The methodology in §7 sequences the empirical work in cost order: cheap measurements first, expensive measurements only on candidates that survive.

## 5. Design axes the field has implicitly varied

A second move of methodology is to enumerate the axes along which the field's 53 artifacts vary. Just as the Babel methodology paper [@rodriguez_babel_2026 §3] identified eight axes the esoteric-language corpus had implicitly used, this paper identifies nine axes the LLM-oriented design space has implicitly used. The axes are interrelated rather than orthogonal, but separating them makes individual design choices reviewable.

**Formatting compactness.** How much of the language's surface is whitespace, comments, type annotations, and other tokens that contribute nothing to AST structure? Python is low-compactness (PEP 8 mandates whitespace); SimPy is high-compactness (formatting stripped). Token Sugar [@sun_token_sugar_2025] generalises the move across languages. The empirical claim of this axis is well-established: -13.5% on CodeLlama and -10.4% on GPT-4 for SimPy versus Python, replicated and extended by Pan et al. [@pan_hiddencost_2025] and ShortenDoc [@shortendoc_2025].

**AST minimalism.** Independent of surface compactness, the underlying abstract syntax tree can be large (rich expressions, many statement forms, complex type system) or small (a handful of forms compose). SimPy preserves Python's full AST; Pel deliberately ships a simpler AST; Quasar [@mcaleese_quasar_2025] adopts a lambda-calculus core. The relationship to LLM behaviour is unclear: a smaller AST is presumably easier for the LLM to learn from limited training data, but may force longer programs to express the same semantics.

**Vocabulary redesign.** How much of the language's keyword and identifier surface has been chosen with LLM tokenizers in mind? SimPy keeps Python's keywords. Pel introduces a new vocabulary. LLMON's tag names are markup-native. The Du-Wang-Wang position paper [@du_wang_wang_2025] argues this axis has been underexploited: vocabulary chosen from the top-frequency BPE-vocabulary entries of the target LLM family would, in principle, ship every keyword in one token.

**Morphological density.** Languages can pack semantic distinctions into surface morphology (Inflexión, Tampio, Latin via Perligata) or distribute them across multi-word constructs (Python, English-keyworded PLs in general). The axis is independent of formatting compactness: a verb-with-clitics in Inflexión is *more* compact than an equivalent Python expression even before any whitespace stripping. The morphbpe finding [@morphbpe_2025] is that BPE tokenization penalises this axis; the SimPy direction takes the penalty for granted; Inflexión's hypothesis [@rodriguez_inflexion_2026 §6] is that the penalty may be locally offset by morpheme-level semantic packing.

**BPE-tokenizer alignment.** Distinct from morphological density, this axis is about whether the language's surface tokens were *chosen* to align with what an LLM's tokenizer already knows. A language with `var` (one BPE token in tiktoken) is aligned; a language with `inmutable_assignment` (multiple tokens) is unaligned. None of the surveyed artifacts make this an explicit design parameter, but Quasar's lambda-calculus core and Pel's uniform grammar both implicitly benefit from it. The B-IR practitioner artifact [@b_ir_2025] takes the move to its extreme: a byte-encoded intent representation that is *intentionally* not human-readable but tokenizer-aligned by construction.

**Grammar regularity.** Natural languages have irregularities, exceptions, and dialect variance; constructed and formal languages have fewer. Tokenese [@youvan_tokenese_2025] argues for high regularity as a design goal; Pel and Quasar build on regular grammars; SimPy preserves Python's regularity. Inflexión, as an inflection-engaging language, sits at lower regularity by design. The axis interacts with morphological density in non-obvious ways: regular morphology (Finnish in Tampio) can be both dense and consistent, where irregular morphology compounds tokenizer fragmentation.

**Ambiguity load.** How much semantic ambiguity does the surface form carry that the LLM must resolve from context? Natural-language-derived syntaxes have higher ambiguity load (the same word means different things in different contexts); typed formal languages can drive ambiguity load to zero. The Mündler-Vechev type-constrained-decoding work [@mundler_type_constrained_2025] is essentially an ambiguity-load reduction technique applied at decode time rather than at language-design time. The Cao-NL-aligned-prover position paper [@cao_nl_aligned_2025] argues the opposite direction: NL-style ambiguity may *help* an LLM by matching its training distribution.

**Human-readability priority.** Where does the language sit on the human-versus-machine readability spectrum? Inform 7 and Python are heavily human-readable; APL [@iverson_notation_1980] and Whitespace are heavily not. The position-paper wave (Kore machine-native [@kore_machine_native_2025], B-IR, ALaS) argues for explicitly deprioritising human readability in LLM-targeted designs. The counter-essay [@codes_mediocrity_2025] argues that LLMs themselves push design toward existing-human-readable conventions because that is what the training data exhibits.

**Ecosystem maturity.** The presence and depth of standard library, package manager, debugger, profiler, documentation culture, idiom community, and decades of human-written code in the language. Python is at the high end; any new LLM-oriented language is at zero. The axis is a Time-amplifier (good ecosystem reduces author / maintainer Time per task) and is the single most often understudied parameter in the LLM-oriented PL literature. Pel, Quasar, NanoLang, and B-IR all start at zero on this axis; none of their empirical claims address what that zero costs in practice.

These nine axes do not exhaust the design space. They are the axes the existing 53 artifacts have implicitly varied. A new design move sometimes corresponds to changing position on one of these axes (SimPy: high on formatting compactness, identical to Python elsewhere); sometimes corresponds to changing position on several jointly (Quasar: AST minimalism low, ambiguity load low, ecosystem maturity zero); and sometimes corresponds to *adding a tenth axis the field has not yet named*. The methodology in §6 puts the schema in place to make those moves reviewable.

## 6. Babel's parameter schema as the methodology lens

The Babel methodology paper [@rodriguez_babel_2026 §4] proposed that an esoteric programming language can be specified, for the purposes of design and generation, as a parameter set across eight mechanical axes (base machine, memory shape, cell width, instruction count and set, encoding, I/O model, additions and removals, theming) plus six meta-parameters describing the language's character rather than its mechanism (complexity, abstraction, verbosity, playfulness, unpredictability, naturalness). The two-layer schema lets a new author meet the design problem at the level of design intent — *I want something playful, high-verbosity, themed around X* — and resolve down to mechanical parameters as a second step.

The methodology presented in this paper extends Babel's schema to the LLM-oriented design space rather than replacing it. The mechanical parameters carry over without modification: an LLM-oriented programming language still has a base machine, a memory shape, a cell width, an instruction set, an encoding, and an I/O model. What needs adding is an **LLM-friendliness meta-parameter cluster** that captures the design intents the existing 53 artifacts implicitly express.

The proposed cluster decomposes into five values:

1. *BPE-token alignment* — to what extent are the language's surface tokens chosen from the target LLM's tokenizer vocabulary? Measurable as tokens-per-keyword averaged over the target model slate. Range: high (every keyword in one token) to low (every keyword fragments).
2. *Token cost per semantic unit* — how many tokens does the language consume to express a unit of semantics (one binding, one function definition, one effectful statement)? Measurable per program. Range: low (Pel, Quasar) to high (Python with PEP 8, Inflexión with full inflection visible).
3. *Generation accuracy* — what one-shot pass rate does a frontier LLM achieve generating this language on a controlled benchmark? Measurable via HumanEval-style benchmark replicated for each candidate language. Range: high (Python, where training data is plentiful) to low (any new language with no training-data exposure).
4. *Comprehension accuracy* — what rate does a frontier LLM achieve answering correctness questions about code in this language? Measurable via a controlled audit benchmark. Range parallels generation accuracy but is distinct (a model may write a language better than it reads it, or vice versa).
5. *Ambiguity load* — what fraction of the language's surface forms have meanings that depend on context the LLM must resolve from prior tokens or from external state? Measurable as the inverse of a grammar's well-typedness ratio, or via direct annotation of a corpus of sample programs. Range: zero (typed formal languages) to high (natural-language-derived syntaxes).

The five LLM-friendliness sub-parameters are not independent. Token cost per semantic unit is partially a function of BPE-token alignment; generation accuracy correlates with ambiguity load via the type-constrained-decoding mechanism [@mundler_type_constrained_2025]. The point of decomposing into five rather than reporting a single LLM-friendliness score is the same point the Babel meta-parameter cluster makes: capturing design intent at a level a new author thinks at, then resolving downward.

The Wenyan-decomposition observation in the Babel methodology paper [@rodriguez_babel_2026 §6] surfaced *naturalness* as a meta-parameter that was too coarse and needed sub-parameters distinguishing vocabulary substitution from lexical-grammar engagement from deep-grammar engagement. The same friction now surfaces *LLM-friendliness*: the existing field treats it as a single dimension, and the dimension is too coarse to compare SimPy (high token-cost win, zero ambiguity-load change) against Pel (high BPE-token-alignment win, full grammar redesign) against Quasar (high generation-accuracy win on its specific workload, no general claim). Making the dimension into five values lets these be compared honestly.

The methodological contribution of this paper is therefore: Babel's schema, extended with the LLM-friendliness cluster, gives the LLM-oriented PL design field a shared vocabulary for what it has been doing implicitly. A reader of any future paper proposing a new LLM-oriented language can ask: *which five values of the cluster did this work move, by how much, on what benchmark?* Answers in that form are reviewable; answers in narrative-only form are not.

## 7. An empirical cascade, sequenced by measurement cost

The methodology has to support: *for a given pair of languages, controlled for task and prompt, measure each stakeholder's efficiency axes and report the Pareto trade-off honestly.* The empirical work is sequenced cheapest-measurement-first, per the difficulty distribution noted in §4: cheap measurements first, expensive measurements only on candidates that survive, gaps documented honestly when measurements are skipped.

**Stage one: free measurements.** Per-LLM token counts of each candidate language for a fixed task suite. SimPy, Pel (if open-source artifact available), Python, Inflexión, and any others are run through the tokenizers of frontier model families — tiktoken's `cl100k_base` and `o200k_base` for the GPT family, the publicly-available tokenizers of Llama 3, Qwen 2.5, Mistral 7B, and others as licensing permits. No model API calls are needed; tokenizer libraries are sufficient. A preliminary run [§3 above] of five short programs across six tokenizers shows SimPy-style stripping at -60% versus Python on average and Inflexión at -28% versus Python on average. The numbers are suggestive but the program suite is too small for general claims; the full empirical study scales to a HumanEval-XL-class suite [@peng_humaneval_xl_2024] of at least 100 tasks.

**Stage two: cheap toolchain instrumentation.** Parse time, compile time, AST size — instrument each language's toolchain and run the same fixed task suite. These measurements answer the compiler-and-interpreter stakeholder's efficiency question directly; they take an afternoon of engineering work and run for free thereafter.

**Stage three: automated LLM benchmarks.** Pass@1 on a HumanEval-XL-class benchmark for each language × LLM cell. A few hundred API calls per cell; total cost in the low hundreds of dollars for a 5-language × 5-model grid. The benchmark must include both generation tasks (LLM writes code) and comprehension tasks (LLM answers questions about provided code). Languages dominated on Stages 1–3 are dropped before the expensive measurements; a language whose token cost is high and whose generation accuracy is low across all models has nothing to gain from human studies.

**Stage four: expensive human studies.** Surviving candidate languages get human-reviewer-Time measurements (controlled audit task with timing), human-author-Time measurements (controlled write task with keystroke logging), and, where the scientific question warrants, human-maintainer-Time measurements via held-out bug-fix suite. The methodological cost-per-data-point here is roughly a hundred times the cost of Stage 3 measurements; the human-study apparatus has to be set up only for languages where the cheap measurements already suggest a real effect.

**Stage five: synthesis and honest reporting.** No claim of *language Y is most efficient* is permitted; the reported result is a two-dimensional Tokens × Time Pareto frontier diagram with the Quality threshold marked, plus the underlying nine-dimensional per-language scores for stakeholder comparison. Gaps where measurements were skipped are reported explicitly; the methodology section names every Stage-4 measurement that was not run and why.

The empirical work is itself part of the contribution. Existing LLM-oriented PL papers each report a context-specific win: SimPy's 13.5% on Python, Quasar's 42% on ViperGPT, TOON's 40% on JSON benchmarks. None of these wins are directly comparable because the contexts differ. A shared cascade, run across a shared task suite, with the candidate languages held constant, produces results that *are* directly comparable, and the comparability is the methodology paper's specific contribution to a field that has accumulated artifacts faster than it has accumulated cross-context evaluation.

The cascade also produces the answer to the gating question of §1. If the Stage 1–3 measurements show that Python plus SimPy (or any other existing combination) dominates the Pareto frontier on the stakeholder profile chosen, the right outcome is to publish this methodology paper as the contribution and *not* build the language proposed for Installment 08. The null result is real, and the discipline of sequencing the cheap measurements first lets the null result be reached without the wasted effort of building a language whose value the empirical work would have negated.
