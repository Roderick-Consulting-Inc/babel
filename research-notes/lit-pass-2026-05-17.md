### Per-artifact score justifications (only the non-obvious ones)

**SimPy.** Formatting compactness is high because the entire design move is whitespace + formatting stripping; AST minimalism is low because the AST is identical to Python's; vocabulary unchanged (low on vocab redesign); BPE alignment scored mid because Python's existing keywords were already designed against English-ASCII frequencies; human-readability dropped from Python's high to mid because PEP-8 cues are gone.

**Pel.** Designed explicitly around constrained LLM generation: small, regular S-expression grammar (high gram-reg), uniform syntax (high AST min), keyword vocabulary chosen for tokenizer-friendliness (high vocab redesign), low ambiguity. Human readability deliberately mid-to-low (Lisp-style is acquired taste). Ecosystem near zero.

**Quasar.** Lambda-calculus core (high AST min), regular grammar (high gram-reg), some vocabulary redesign but emphasis is on smaller AST not keyword choice (mid vocab). Ambiguity low because of type system + uncertainty bounds. Python-Quasar transpile means partial Python-ecosystem reuse, but Quasar itself is fresh (low).

**LLMON.** Formatting compactness high vs JSON/Markdown alternatives by design; vocab redesign high (custom tags); LLMON is markup, so AST min n/a (the relevant analogue would be schema complexity, which is mid).

**NanoLang.** Practitioner ecosystem mirror of Pel: prefix notation, mandatory test blocks, transpiles to C; high compactness, high AST min, high vocab redesign, high BPE alignment (prefix operator tokens are single BPE tokens), high gram-reg, low ambiguity, low ecosystem.

**B-IR.** The HN comment thread (not the artifact itself) describes design moves consistent with high compactness, high vocab redesign, high BPE alignment; the artifact is intentionally not human-readable (low HumRead) and has no ecosystem.

**Tokenese.** Constructed natural language (not a PL); scored where the axes still apply by analogy — high vocab redesign, high BPE alignment, high gram-reg, low morph density (deliberately analytic), zero ecosystem. Iverson-style "ambiguity drive to zero" the explicit goal.

**TOON.** Behaves like LLMON on the markup axes: high compactness vs JSON, high BPE alignment (tabular row encoding lets identifiers tokenise once), low ambiguity, mid human-readability (still legible).

**PDL.** YAML base means low compactness on the format itself, but high AST min (small set of block types), high gram-reg, high human-readability — closer to DSPy's "framework not language" profile.

**Plang.** Blends NL with control-flow markers, so ambiguity load is high by design, human-readability is high (the explicit design goal), formatting compactness is low (NL is verbose), vocab redesign is low (uses ordinary English).

**LMQL / DSPy / SGLang / APPL / Pangolin / Wang effect-handler.** All "language-that-runs-LLMs" artifacts — they don't redesign keyword surface (low vocab redesign), inherit a host language's compactness profile, and their key dimensions of variation are *enforcement-locus* and *effect-system* rather than tokenizer alignment. They score similarly on the 9 axes despite expressing very different design ideas — which is a signal that the 9 axes don't fully characterise this family (see §4 below).

**CNL-P.** Controlled NL means low compactness (NL is verbose), high human-readability, low ambiguity (strict grammar), low vocab redesign (uses ordinary English).

**Type-Constrained Codegen.** As a decoder-time constraint, it reduces the *target language's* ambiguity load (low) without changing any of the other axes.

## Section 4: Axis gap analysis

The audit confirms three or more artifacts the existing 9 axes cannot cleanly describe. Two distinct gaps surface; a 10th axis is justified for the first, and an 11th would be defensible if the field grows further on the second.

### Proposed 10th axis: **Enforcement locus**

**Definition.** Where in the LLM pipeline is the language's contract checked / enforced?

- **author-time** — type-checker / linter runs against the human-or-LLM-author's source before generation finishes (Python's PEP 484, Pel, NanoLang, Pangolin's effect-handler types)
- **decode-time** — constraint is encoded as a grammar / type automaton against which the decoder is constrained at every token (XGrammar, Grammar-Aligned Decoding, Type-Constrained Codegen, LMQL constraints)
- **parse-time** — generated output is parsed and rejected/repaired after generation (DSPy's signature validation, SGLang's compressed FSM checks)
- **runtime** — contract is checked only on execution (Quasar's conformal-prediction uncertainty bounds, Pangolin's selection-monad assertions)

A given artifact may sit at **multiple loci simultaneously** (this is the set-valued observation Babel's 2026-05-13 audit flagged at E4): Quasar enforces at parse-time *and* runtime; XGrammar at decode-time *and* parse-time; APPL at author-time *and* parse-time. The existing 9 axes have no slot for "where the contract bites," and the field is increasingly differentiated on exactly this dimension.

**Two-or-more-artifacts test (met).** XGrammar, Type-Constrained Codegen, and Grammar-Aligned Decoding are *only* describable as "PL design at the decoder boundary"; they have no AST/vocab/morph/grammar surface of their own — they are a position on the *enforcement-locus* axis applied to whatever language is the target. Quasar's conformal prediction is similarly a runtime-locus design choice unrelated to its lambda-calculus core. Pangolin's selection-monad / effect-handler split is an enforcement-locus claim before it is anything else.

**How to interpret on the 9-axis table.** Decoder-boundary artifacts currently appear with rows of "n/a" because they fail every existing-axis question; an enforcement-locus axis would give them a real position to occupy, and would also re-score author-time vs runtime PL-internal artifacts in a way that distinguishes LMQL (decode-time + author-time) from DSPy (parse-time) from Pangolin (runtime + author-time).

### Second candidate gap (not yet at the two-artifact threshold): **Artifact kind / scope**

A second, weaker gap is that the 9 axes assume "a language" — but the field contains:
- whole languages (Pel, Quasar, NanoLang, Tokenese)
- whole markup formats (TOON, LLMON, PDL)
- transpilers / rewriters (SimPy, Token Sugar, ShortCoder, ShortenDoc)
- host-embedded DSLs (LMQL, DSPy, SGLang, APPL, Pangolin, Wang effect-handler)
- decoder-boundary constraint systems (XGrammar, GAD, Type-Constrained Codegen)
- position papers / surveys (Du-Wang-Wang, Cao NL-aligned, Kore, kirancodes, Vibe Reasoning, Reasoning as Resource)
- empirical studies (Let Me Speak Freely, LLMs Love Python, Scaling Laws Code, Hidden Cost, MorphBPE, TokDrift, Hannecke)

The existing 9 axes implicitly assume the first category and degrade gracefully into "n/a" for the others. An *artifact-kind* axis would not capture a design choice (so it isn't a true design-axis), but would let the field carve out coherent sub-fields. Because this is a categorical taxonomy rather than a continuous design axis, **the agent does not recommend adding it as a 10th axis** — it belongs in the §6 schema layer of the methodology paper as an artifact-kind classifier, not in the §5 design-axis cluster.

**Recommendation.** Add **enforcement_locus** as a 10th design axis (set-valued, with author-time / decode-time / parse-time / runtime as the canonical values). Keep artifact-kind as a separate classifier surfaced in the schema (E4 in the 2026-05-13 audit handles this already).

## Section 5: Methodology notes

**Search strategy.** Heavy WebSearch on arXiv + ACL Anthology + ACM Digital Library + conf.researchr.org for the LMPL 2025 / 2026 program pages + GitHub for practitioner artifacts + Medium/HN for essay-class artifacts. Each seed key was searched with a 1–2-keyword combination derived from the BibTeX `title` field. Where a search returned the canonical preprint, the paper landing page was confirmed; where it returned only secondary mentions, the agent followed through to the primary source.

**Verification standard.** A seed entry was marked VERIFIED if the agent surfaced (a) the title at a primary URL (arXiv, ACL Anthology, conference page, or the artifact's own canonical repo) and (b) at least one named author. Where the seed bib's author list was vague ("Tan and others", "Dong and others"), the corrected author list was added. Where dates differed from the seed bib (e.g. LMQL 2024 vs PLDI 2023), the canonical earliest publication date was used.

**What couldn't be found.**
- **MLDS** (`mlds_2025`). No 2025 position paper matching the seed description ("Machine-Learning Domain-Specific Languages as a Class") was located. The agent searched for both the literal title and several plausible alternatives. Treat as unverified — it may have been a notional placeholder when the seed list was drafted.
- **Pangolin authorship.** The seed bib attributes Pangolin to "Tan and others"; the canonical author is **Shangyin Tan** (Berkeley PhD), with co-authors Guannan Wei, Koushik Sen, Matei Zaharia. Corrected.
- **xu_llm_preferences_2025 authorship.** The canonical first author of "LLMs Love Python" is **Luke Twist** (GitHub: itsluketwist), not "Xu". The agent retained the seed key for citation continuity but flags the discrepancy.
- **B-IR.** The Hacker News post (`46583581`) and GitHub repo (`imjasonh`) are real, but the design described in the seed bib ("byte-encoded intent representation") does not appear in the actual artifact text. B-IR is closer to a "validation-locality LLM target language" experiment than to a byte-encoded representation. The seed entry overstates what was built.
- **Kore.** The seed bib's "Kore Authors" attribution resolved to **Padmaraj Kore**'s Medium essay — a single-author practitioner essay, not a multi-author collective. Title: "Rethinking Programming Languages for LLMs: Building a Machine-Native Language."
- **du_wang_wang_2025.** Resolved as the LMPL 2025 position paper by Yalong Du / Chaozheng Wang / Huaijin Wang — same key, but the seed description ("vocabulary from top-frequency BPE entries") is not actually the paper's thesis. The paper's thesis is broader (integrate PL techniques into LLM code generation generally). Corrected.

**What I deliberately skipped.**
- Going deeper into adjacent fields (specifically: LLM-for-formal-methods, LLM-for-program-repair, LLM-driven IDE work) — these have huge literatures but are not in the LLM-oriented PL design space the methodology paper is focused on.
- Per-paper PDF extraction for the LMPL 2025 individual papers (the proceedings TOC + conference page gave enough information for axis mapping; full-text reads would be Stage-2 work).
- Searching for a hypothetical 53rd artifact list — the seed bib has 36 distinct keys, and the methodology paper claims "at least 53"; this agent surfaced 51 distinct verifiable artifacts (36 seed minus 1 unverified MLDS + 20 new = roughly the same total), suggesting the "53" figure is plausible if NanoLang, the LMPL 2025 long tail, and TokDrift/Grammar-Based Code Repr are counted.
- Time-boxed at roughly 75 minutes; the LMPL 2026 accepted-papers page was empty (CFP only); ICLR/ACL/EMNLP/ISSTA/PLDI 2026 spring searches returned mostly DSPy-derivative and orchestration-derivative practitioner artifacts (e.g. Agent Capsules arXiv 2605.00410) that are downstream of the methodology paper's central concern and don't shift the axis analysis.

**Caveats.** The 9-axis scoring is per the agent's reading of the verified metadata and a small number of artifact-page reads; for any artifact where the score is load-bearing for a downstream methodology decision, the human author should spot-check the row against the primary source. The proposed enforcement-locus 10th axis is the only structural addition to the methodology paper this lit pass recommends; the other observations refine seed-bib metadata rather than the schema.
