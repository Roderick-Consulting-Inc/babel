Style: Chicago author-date via pandoc + BibTeX. Voice: essayistic for curious minds. Honest preparation by default — hedged novelty claims, lineage credited, counter-evidence cited.*

## 1. Two questions

The first installment of this series [@rodriguez_babel_2026] argued that the esoteric-programming-language field had produced a vast corpus and a vague methodology: roughly 1,500 catalogued languages and ~800 Brainfuck derivatives, with each author rebuilding the same scaffolding because no shared parameter schema existed. Babel proposed that schema. The second installment, *Inflexión* [@rodriguez_inflexion_2026], developed one specific instantiation — a hand-built esoteric programming language whose semantics flow from Rioplatense Argentine Spanish grammar — and joined a small lineage of inflection-driven non-English natural-language esolangs (Perligata, Espro, Tampio, Wenyan, Inflexión). Both papers are written *for humans*. The languages they discuss are evaluated by what they do for human readers, writers, and the small community of practitioners who care about esoteric programming languages as a research and cultural genre.

This third installment is about a parallel field that has emerged on a different schedule and for a different audience. Between Sun, Du, Yang, Li, and Lo's ISSTA 2024 paper *AI Coders Are Among Us* [@sun_simpy_2024] and the present moment, at least 53 distinct artifacts have been built whose target audience is *large language models*. SimPy [@sun_simpy_2024] strips Python's formatting tokens to cut LLM-input cost by 13.5% on CodeLlama and 10.4% on GPT-4. Pel [@mohammadi_pel_2025] designs a new programming language from scratch with a uniform grammar 'to facilitate easier learning (in-context) and reliable generation by LLMs.' Quasar [@mcaleese_quasar_2025] reports 42% time and 52% security improvements on ViperGPT with a lambda-calculus-cored language optimised for LLM-agent code actions. LLMON [@hind_llmon_2026] proposes an LLM-native markup at the structured-data interface. The LMPL workshop at SPLASH — *Language Models and Programming Languages* — held its first edition in Singapore in 2025 and is accepting submissions for its second edition in Oakland for October 2026 [@lmpl_2026_cfp]. The field has a name and a venue.

The pace and the scale together raise two questions, both of which this paper takes seriously.

**The first question is the design question:**

> *What does the most efficient programming language look like, considering it needs to be understood, written, vetted, or at least auditable by humans; easy to understand and implement by machines; and easy for LLMs to read and generate?*

The question's structure matters. "Most efficient" is not a single-axis optimisation. SimPy is most efficient on *LLM-token-economy-of-Python-code*. Inflexión is most efficient on *morphological-density-of-grammar-derived-semantics*. APL [@iverson_notation_1980] is most efficient on *expression-density-per-glyph*. Python is most efficient on *human-author-velocity-with-tooling-ecosystem*. None dominates the others on all axes. The design question reduces, honestly stated, to: *which corner of a multi-stakeholder Pareto frontier is worth occupying, and how would one know?*

**The second question, equally load-bearing, is the gating question:**

> *Does building a new programming language actually add value over the existing positions on the frontier — Python plus SimPy, Pel, Quasar, LLMON, and the rest — or would the exercise reduce to pretending to be cool with accepted techs?*

This is the honest-preparation discipline applied to the project itself. Installment 05's lineage correction [@rodriguez_inflexion_2026 §1] reframed Inflexión from *first ever* to *fifth in a small lineage* because a pre-publication literature check surfaced four prior authors. The same discipline applies here, one level up: the decision to *exist as a new language* is something the empirical work must justify, not something that exists by default. The null result is real and acceptable. If the empirical comparison shows existing artifacts already cover the Pareto frontier on the chosen-stakeholder profile, the right outcome is to ship this paper as a methodology contribution and stop. No new language. The temptation to add another entry to a 53-artifact field because the surrounding research is interesting is exactly the trap to avoid.

The methodology proposed here is the framework that makes the design choices explicit and the gating decision empirically grounded. Babel's parameter schema, applied to the LLM-oriented design space, gives the field a shared vocabulary for what it has been varying implicitly. The empirical cascade laid out in §7 lets a researcher answer the gating question in cost order — cheapest measurements first, expensive measurements only on candidates that survive — and report the result honestly, with explicit gaps where measurements were skipped.

## 2. The field as it stands, 2024–2026

A pre-publication literature pass surfaced 53 artifacts in the LLM-oriented programming-language design space, with broad coverage across arXiv, ACL Anthology, ACM venues, the LMPL workshop programmes, the IBM and Stanford open-source repositories, and the practitioner ecosystem. The full bibliography is in the references; the structural observation worth making here is that **two distinct families have been converging under the same banner**.

The first family is *language LLMs write* — programming languages whose grammar has been redesigned so the language model itself generates code more cheaply, more reliably, or both. SimPy [@sun_simpy_2024] is the foundational example. Token Sugar [@sun_token_sugar_2025] generalises the move to other languages. The Hidden Cost of Readability [@pan_hiddencost_2025] quantifies the formatting tax across multiple LLMs. ShortCoder [@liu_shortcoder_2026] does syntax-level optimisation with rule-based rewriting plus LLM-guided refinement. NanoLang, B-IR, and ALaS are practitioner artifacts in the same direction: small languages whose author intent is *what an LLM will emit, not what a human will type*. Pel [@mohammadi_pel_2025] takes the further step of designing a new programming language from scratch under this constraint. Quasar [@mcaleese_quasar_2025] adds a lambda-calculus core and conformal-prediction reliability bounds.

The second family is *language that runs LLMs* — programming languages whose programs *call* language models as part of their semantics. LMQL [@lmql_2024] is the grandparent. DSPy [@dspy_2025] adds typed signatures and a compiler-style optimiser. SGLang [@sglang_2025] is co-designed PL plus runtime. APPL [@dong_appl_2025] embeds prompts in Python with an async runtime. PDL [@vaziri_pdl_2025] is IBM's YAML-based declarative prompt language. Plang [@hu_plang_2025] blends natural-language prompt with control-flow markers. Pangolin [@tan_pangolin_2025] makes best-of-N and retries first-class via the selection monad. Effect-handler-based prompt programming [@wang_lmpl_2025] separates LLM-script logic from effects. These languages do not strip or redesign anything for the *target* LLM; they design infrastructure for *invoking* LLMs.

Adjacent to both families, but conceptually distinct, is the markup-and-data-format axis. LLMON [@hind_llmon_2026], TOON [@toon_spec_2025], and PDL [@vaziri_pdl_2025] target the structured-data boundary rather than the executable-code boundary. Tokenese [@youvan_tokenese_2025] takes the move all the way out of programming and proposes a *natural language* designed for AI tokenization. JSON-vs-YAML-vs-Markdown-vs-TOON practitioner benchmarks [@hannecke_format_2025] measure the *output*-side formatting tax that complements SimPy's *input*-side observation.

Grammar-constrained and type-constrained decoding is the silent PL-design lever of 2025. XGrammar [@dong_xgrammar_2024], Type-Constrained Code Generation [@mundler_type_constrained_2025] at PLDI 2025, Grammar-Aligned Decoding [@park_grammar_aligned_2024], and the rest of this cluster do not call themselves "PL design," but a context-free grammar or type system attached to the decoder *is* a programming-language contract negotiated with the model. The field would be incomplete without these.

Finally, the position-paper wave: Du-Wang-Wang [@du_wang_wang_2025], CNL-P [@xing_cnlp_2025], MLDS [@mlds_2025], the *NL-aligned theorem prover* call by Cao, Xie, and Yan [@cao_nl_aligned_2025], the Kore *machine-native language* essay [@kore_machine_native_2025], and several Medium-published practitioner essays are all arguing variants of the same thesis in independent vocabularies: *programming-language design should be reshaped around LLM cognition rather than human cognition*. The thesis converges across disciplines without yet having a shared ontology.

Counter-evidence runs alongside the convergence. *Let Me Speak Freely* [@tam_let_me_speak_2024] shows that strict structured-output formats can *degrade* LLM reasoning quality — the opposite of the SimPy-direction intuition. *Scaling Laws for Code* [@scaling_laws_code_2026] gives a predictability ordering across existing programming languages (roughly C# < Java ≈ Rust < Go < TypeScript < JavaScript < Python) and argues that language structure changes LLM scaling behaviour in measurable, predictable ways — suggesting that picking the *right existing language* might dominate inventing a new one. The *kirancodes* essay [@codes_mediocrity_2025] argues, from a critical position, that LLMs push programming-language design toward conservative, popular syntaxes rather than novel ones; the wider study of LLM programming-language preferences [@xu_llm_preferences_2025] supports the empirical half of that claim. The Bayazit-Li LMPL paper [@bayazit_proof_2025] examines a pre-existing programming language (a proof assistant) as an LLM target and informs the *PL-was-already-good-for-LLMs* axis the position-paper wave largely ignores.

The field, by 2026, contains at least 53 distinct artifacts across these axes, with no consensus framework, no shared evaluation methodology, and active counter-evidence that the field's central direction may be wrong. **That is the situation a methodology paper has to make sense of.**

## 3. The Youvan / Inflexión tension, resolved empirically

The simplest version of the field's central claim — *natural-language morphology is inefficient for AI tokenization, so AI-oriented languages should be more regular and more compact than natural-language-derived ones* — is what Youvan defends explicitly in *Tokenese* [@youvan_tokenese_2025] and what the SimPy direction assumes implicitly. The simplest counter — *natural-language morphology can pack semantic distinctions tighter than English-keyworded syntax, so engaging it is worth the tokenization cost* — is what Inflexión [@rodriguez_inflexion_2026 §3.1] embodies in the *ser* / *estar* mapping. *Ser* and *estar* together carry the immutable-versus-mutable binding distinction in a single morpheme that Python expresses (where it expresses it at all) through a five-token `Final[int] = 5` annotation, which is optional and not enforced at the binding site.

Both observations are correct on their narrow axes:

| | Natural-language morphology brings | |
|---|---|---|
| | **Wins** | **Losses** |
| For humans familiar with the language | Locally rich: one morpheme carries the whole distinction without a separate annotation | Irregularities, exceptions, dialect variance; learning curve for outsiders |
| For LLMs under byte-pair encoding | Frequent forms tokenize cheaply (one or two tokens); semantic density at the surface | Rare and inflected forms fragment into multi-token sequences [@morphbpe_2025]; BPE was trained on analytic-language frequency |

The net effect is empirical, not theoretical. Whether morphological density's local wins dominate its tokenizer-tail losses on a given workload depends on the workload, on the tokenizer's vocabulary, on the LLM's training-data exposure to the substrate language, and on which stakeholder's efficiency is being measured. The first installment of the empirical methodology proposed in §7 takes a small fixed program suite and tokenizes Python, SimPy-style Python, and Inflexión versions of each across six frontier tokenizers; preliminary results (averaged over five programs, six tokenizers) show Inflexión consuming **-28% tokens versus Python** with a per-program range of -61.8% to +8.9%. The result is suggestive, not conclusive — four of the five Inflexión samples use design-level syntax not yet implemented in the runtime, and the suite is too small to reject either direction's thesis. Conclusive results require the full empirical cascade [§7] run across a larger task suite with more LLM models. The point worth making in this section is structural: the Youvan position and the Inflexión position are not in conflict; they describe different points on the same two-dimensional trade-off. The field's open empirical question is which point wins on a given workload, not which position is right in the abstract.

The same point applies symmetrically to the wider field. SimPy's whitespace-stripping wins are measured on Python source; whether they transfer to a Rust or Haskell target is unstudied. Pel's uniform-grammar wins are measured on LLM-agent code-action workloads; whether they transfer to general programming is unstudied. Quasar's 42-percent-time / 52-percent-security wins are measured on ViperGPT and ScienceQA; whether they transfer to SWE-bench-style multi-step agent workloads is unstudied. Every existing artifact's central empirical claim is local to its measurement context, and the field has no shared cross-context benchmark. The methodology proposed in §6–§7 addresses exactly this gap.
